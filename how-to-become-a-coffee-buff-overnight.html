    8 steps to help you become a coffee buff overnight

    Enjoy your caffeine fix but want to know if you're really getting the best coffee you can? We spoke to Jeffrey Young, founder of London Coffee Festival, to find out how to become a coffee buff overnight, and his tips for brewing professional-grade beverages at home....

    1. Keep your coffee in a cool dark place and not in the fridge. This will stop it from losing moisture.

    2. Use bottled water. This will help the purity of your coffee and extract more flavour during brewing.

    3. Pay close attention to the quality of the coffee itself. Try to use coffee that has been roasted as recently as possible. The more recently it was roasted the fresher it is and this will make you a far better cup. Keep an eye on the "Roasted on" date when buying.

    Related Story

    4. Buy wholebean coffee and grind it yourself, rather than pre-ground coffee. But make sure you get the right one for your preferred brewing method - some require a coarser or a finer grind. It should also be done when the beans are as fresh as possible. You can pick up a home burr grinder to grind your beans as and when you need them, which will make a marked difference to your morning cup and ensure even particles for better extraction.

    5. Find the right brewing method for you. There are so many ways to brew coffee and they will all produce different flavours and styles. Espresso and French press are perhaps the classic, but there has been a rise in recent years of newer methods such as the AeroPress and V60. Both of these brew fantastic coffees perfect for any time of day and that really bring out the flavours of each coffee.

    6. Experiment. If you want to be a real coffee buff, buy a small notebook and keep it by your coffee machine so that you can keep track of which ones you've tried and what you thought.

    Related Story

    7. Shop around. Most good coffee shops will sell bags of the coffee they use in their espressos - you just need to ask. These are usually sold as whole beans so be sure to ask them to grind it if you don't have your own grinder.

    8. Pay attention to where your coffee came from. Origin coffee is going to be increasingly noticeable this year as people search for their favourite cup. In much the same way that people pay attention to the grapes that go into their wine, people will do the same for what coffee goes into their flat whites. With coffee grown all round the world in the so-called coffee belt (between the tropics of Cancer and Capricorn), there is huge variation between every variety.

    A great first step is to understand the key characteristics of the three regions.

    • Latin America – bright and higher in acidity, Latin American coffees tend to have a lower body than the rest of the world.
    • Africa and Arabia – medium bodied and typically fruity or floral, coffees from this region are the oldest coffees in the world.
    • Asia Pacific – fuller bodied coffees with deeper aromas such as herbs and spices.
