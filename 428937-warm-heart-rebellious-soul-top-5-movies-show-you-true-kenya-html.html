    What does it mean to be Kenyan?

    The key to understanding a culture is being able to speak, read, and understand their language. This way, you can comprehend news, literature, and the history of a particular place. It’s the gateway to learning about the people or a nation.

    But many people don’t yet have the requisite language skills of reading high-level material in foreign languages. That’s why movies and TV shows can become a secondary way to “know” and understand a culture.

    If you want to know the people of Kenya, you may want to start at the political landscape, but that can seem overwhelming for most – yet that’s not the accurate picture of the real Kenyan.

    It took a long time to get to a point where Kenyans can produce their films that portray the true spirit of a Kenyan and our culture – and there’s still a long way to go.

    If you think of a movie about Kenya, you might think of a local comedy show like Vitimbi. But several movies show the authentic Kenyan culture and character without being overly patriotic.

    Nairobi Half Life is a story about Mwas – a dreamer from upcountry who has wild ambitions about making it big in Nairobi, the capital city of Kenya. In pursuit of his big plans and much to the chagrin of his family, he heads to Nairobi.

    Mwas gets mugged on arriving in Nairobi and quickly discovers the harsh reality of city life. Mwas gets into crime to sustain himself, but the gang he hangs out with the most gets killed by the cops tragically. From here on, he purposes to chase his acting dream instead.

    This movie shows the indomitable spirit of a Kenyan when faced with insurmountable challenges – they always find their way back to their path of destiny.

    Something Necessary is a movie about Anne, who is at a pivotal period in her life. Following the 2007 elections that swept Kenya, leaving a trail of destruction in its path, Anne struggles to rebuild her life one day at a time.

    Having lost her husband during the post-election skirmishes, Anne, now a single mom and widow, must take care of her sickly son and still deal with the young gangster who is drawn to her.

    This movie shows the resilience of Kenyan women in the face of uncertainty. She always prevails and displays a hard work ethic, multitasking abilities, and a fighting spirit.

    Vote for Kibera is a documentary film that follows the people living in Africa’s largest slum. Despite the harsh conditions they live in, they’re determined to transform the merciless environment into a better place to live.

    The movie paints a different and fresh portrait of the slum that’s rarely seen in many similar films that seek to tell the Kibera story.

    Throughout the film, you can see the hard work ethic, undying spirit, hope, and creativity of a place that’s long been riddled with stories of despair and poverty. You’ll also get to see how the people rebel against the status quo to transform their community and improve their lives and those of the people around them.

    Actor Innocent Njuguna plays the lead role along with award-winning Kenyan superstar artist Avril.

    In this movie, the two star-crossed lovers must fight for their love. Avril plays a young woman from the slums, while Innocent is a rich man engaged to the perfect woman.

    However, their paths cross, and soon, they’re faced with fighting for their relationship.

    The movie depicts Kenyans’ warm hearts, transcending social status, class, and other hindrances impacting relationships.

    Jane and Abel, acted by Joyce Maina and Helena Waithera, star in this original Kenyan film as two best friends who struggle to make ends meet. They hop from one job to another – one day selling scratch and win tickets, the other day they’re pulling an all-nighter writing sample scripts in the hope of scoring a film gig.

    Sadly, Tande takes Zahara’s script, submits it as hers, and gets the gig. But karma knows no ambition – it has a way of snatching dreams, especially if they were stolen in the first place.

    This movie also reveals the true fighting spirit of a Kenyan where no challenge is too difficult to deal with. They face things head-on, step-by-step, but finally, they emerge victorious in their quest.
